Why this matters

When a service firm crosses 11 employees, admin load stops being a founder problem and becomes a margin problem. The firms I work with typically have 6 to 9 people touching a single client deliverable before it goes out the door. Each handoff adds 20–45 minutes of copy-paste, reformatting, and status updates that clients never see and never pay for.

The instinct is to hire a coordinator. But a new hire at $55,000/year only absorbs about 30 hours of admin per week before they generate their own admin downstream — Slack threads, 1:1s, approvals, onboarding for the next hire. You're running up a down escalator.

The firms that cut admin time by 40% don't automate everything. They kill 60% of the work first, then automate what's left. Sequence matters.

1. Map the admin that's actually killing you

Most firms skip this step and jump straight to "we need a better tool." That's prescribing medication before running the diagnosis. Before you buy software or hire anyone, you need a week of honest data.

The 5-day audit

Pick one week. Every team member logs admin tasks in a shared sheet with four columns: task, minutes, frequency, and who could do it instead (human, automation, client self-serve, or "kill entirely"). Be specific. "Admin" is not a task. "Reformatting the Tuesday client report in Google Docs" is a task.

Multiply the four scores. Anything above 60 goes on the priority list. Anything above 100 is an emergency — you're bleeding hours on something a well-configured workflow could eat in seconds.

What you're looking for

  • Repeated data movement: the same information entered into 2+ systems.
  • Reformatting work: taking content that already exists and making it look different.
  • Status-chasing: Slack messages, emails, or meetings whose only purpose is "where are we on X?"
  • Template assembly: building proposals, reports, or onboarding packs from known components.
If a task can be described as a recipe — "when X happens, take Y from system A and put it in system B in format C" — it should not be on a human's plate.

2. Kill before you automate

Here's the mistake that costs firms six months and $30,000 in consulting fees: they automate a broken process. Now the bad process runs faster and at scale. You've multiplied the mess.

Before anything gets automated, every task on your priority list goes through a four-question filter. If the answer to any of the first three is yes, don't automate it — remove it.

The kill filter

  1. Does anyone actually use the output? The weekly 12-page internal report. The status dashboard nobody opens. Pull the logs. If fewer than 3 people have viewed it in the last 60 days, kill it.
  2. Could the client do this themselves in 90 seconds? Intake forms, scheduling, document uploads, approval clicks. Self-serve is cheaper and faster than any automation you'll build.
  3. Is this a workaround for a missing feature in an existing tool? Sometimes the fix is a $29/month upgrade, not a custom workflow.
  4. Does this need to happen at all? A lot of admin is organizational scar tissue from a problem that stopped existing 18 months ago.

3. Automate what survives — in the right order

Now you automate. But sequence is everything, because every automation you build creates a small maintenance load. Build the wrong ones first and you'll spend more time babysitting workflows than you saved.

The automation priority stack

  1. Data movement between systems you already pay for. Highest ROI, lowest risk. Your CRM talking to your project management tool. Your intake form populating your onboarding checklist. These are plumbing — boring, reliable, transformative.
  2. Document and deliverable generation. Proposals, onboarding packs, client reports, invoices. Templates + data + an AI pass for personalization. Typical time cut: 80–95%.
  3. Status and reminder workflows. Nudges, escalations, overdue alerts, check-ins. Kills meetings, kills Slack threads, kills the "did anyone follow up?" question.
  4. AI-assisted review and summarization. Call transcripts into CRM notes. Long email threads into action items. Last because it requires the previous layers to have clean data to work with.

4. The $55k coordinator test

Before you greenlight any automation project, run it through the coordinator test. Would you hire a $55,000/year coordinator to do only this work? If yes, the automation is worth building at almost any reasonable price. If no, you're over-investing.

Most firms I audit have 3–5 workflows that easily clear a full coordinator's workload on their own. Onboarding automation. Proposal generation. Weekly reporting. Each one reclaims 8–14 hours per week of senior-level time — time that used to get swallowed by work that doesn't require judgment.

Typical ROI timelines

  • Month 1: Build and test. Net time cost, not savings. Expect 15–25 hours of stakeholder time during build.
  • Month 2: Breakeven on the first two workflows. Team starts trusting the system.
  • Month 3: 8–12 hours/week recovered. The complaints shift from "I'm drowning" to "can we automate X too?"
  • Month 6: 18–27 hours/week recovered across the team. Margin improves by 4–8 points on comparable revenue.

5. Governance, or: how not to create a new mess

Automation without governance is just chaos running faster. Three rules keep it in check.

  • One owner per workflow. Every automation has a named human who gets paged when it breaks. No owner, no workflow.
  • Logs before launch. Every run writes to a log table with inputs, outputs, and timestamps. When a client asks "did you send that?" you answer in 10 seconds, not 30 minutes.
  • Quarterly prune. Every 90 days, review what's running. Kill anything that hasn't fired in 60 days or whose output nobody uses. Workflows accumulate like tabs — they need a cleanup pass.

A minimal logging pattern

workflow_runs
- run_id (uuid)
- workflow_name (text)
- triggered_at (timestamp)
- input_summary (jsonb)
- output_summary (jsonb)
- status (success | failed | partial)
- error_message (text, nullable)
- human_reviewer (text, nullable)

Six columns. A simple Supabase table. That's the difference between an automation program you trust and one that scares you.

6. What to do in the next 7 days

Reading this guide recovers zero hours. Here's the week-one action plan that actually moves the number.

  1. Day 1 (Monday): Announce the audit to your team. Set up the shared tracking sheet. Tell people the goal is to find and kill admin, not to measure their performance.
  2. Days 2–5: Team logs admin tasks in real time. You do it too — owners are often the worst offenders.
  3. Day 6 (Saturday): Score every task using the rubric in Section 1. Sort by score.
  4. Day 7 (Sunday): Run the top 15 tasks through the kill filter. Make three lists: Kill, Self-serve, Automate. Pick the top 2 automation candidates.

What good looks like after week one

  • 3–7 tasks killed outright. Usually reports nobody reads and meetings that could have been async.
  • 2–4 tasks moved to self-serve. Client-facing forms, scheduling links, document uploads.
  • 2 automation candidates scoped. Each with a clear trigger, clear output, and a named owner.
  • A baseline number. Total admin hours/week across the team. You can't improve what you don't measure.

7. The honest limits of this framework

I'll tell you where this doesn't work, because nobody else will.

  • Under 11 employees: You probably don't have enough process repetition yet. Wait until you have at least 3 people doing similar admin work.
  • Over 100 employees: You need change management, not just workflow design. The technical work is the easy part; getting 8 department heads aligned is where projects die.
  • Heavily regulated work: Automating it is possible but the audit and compliance layer triples the build cost. Worth it, but budget accordingly.
  • Chaotic process cultures: If every client engagement is a snowflake with no repeatable pattern, fix the delivery model first. Automation can't create structure that doesn't exist.
Automation is a multiplier, not a fix. It amplifies whatever's already there — including the dysfunction. Clean up first, then turn the volume up.

The hard part isn't identifying the opportunity. It's sequencing the work so you don't create a second mess on top of the first.
