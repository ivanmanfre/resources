Why This Workflow Matters

Here's what I see with agencies and ghostwriters managing multiple clients:

You hop off a strategy call. Good call, lots of insights. Client talked about their positioning, dropped some golden phrases, mentioned competitors, shared what's working and what's not.

Then you spend the next 2-4 hours:
  • Scrubbing through the transcript to find the good stuff
  • Matching insights to the client's brand voice guidelines
  • Formatting everything into a content brief your team can actually use
  • Repeating this for every client, every week
That's 8-16 hours a week on extraction work. Not strategy. Not creative. Just pulling information from one place and putting it in another.

This workflow does that entire process automatically. Call ends, transcript lands in your system, AI extracts the insights, matches it against the client's voice profile, and delivers a ready-to-use content brief to your project management tool.

Detailed Workflow Build

Node 1: Webhook Trigger

What it does: Receives the transcript when your meeting tool processes a new recording.

Configuration:
  • Webhook URL: Your n8n instance generates this
  • Authentication: Header auth with API key
  • HTTP Method: POST
Connect this to: Fireflies, Otter, or whatever transcription tool you use. Most have Zapier or webhook integrations. Point them at this URL.

Data you're receiving:
`json
{
"meeting_id": "abc123",
"client_name": "Client XYZ",
"transcript": "Full transcript text...",
"duration": "45 minutes",
"date": "2024-01-15",
"participants": ["You", "Client Contact"]
}
`

Tip: If your transcription tool doesn't have a native webhook, set up a Zapier or Make trigger that watches for new transcripts and sends them to your n8n webhook.

Node 2: Transcript Cleanup (Code Node)

What it does: Cleans up the raw transcript, removes filler, and structures the conversation into usable chunks.

Why this matters: Raw transcripts are messy. "Um," "uh," speaker labels mixed in, timestamp artifacts. The AI works better with clean input.
