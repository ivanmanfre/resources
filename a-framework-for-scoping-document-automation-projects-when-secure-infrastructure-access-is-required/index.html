Why This Matters

Here is the failure pattern I watch repeat across insurance brokerages, construction firms, and property management offices: the automation logic takes 2-4 days to build. The infrastructure access to reach the documents takes 2-4 weeks to untangle. That ratio is not a bug in one project, it is the default shape of every document automation engagement over $10,000.

When a vendor quotes you $15,000 for "automating your signing workflow" and the project lands at $24,000 eight weeks late, the overrun is almost never the automation. It is the VPN tunnel your IT team had to provision, the service account your insurance software vendor took 11 business days to approve, or the remote desktop instance someone had to stand up because the documents only open on a specific machine.

The automation is the last 20% of the work. The access layer is the first 80%, and it is the part nobody scopes until the quote is already signed.

This guide is the scoping framework I run with every prospective client before a statement of work gets drafted. It takes 30-45 minutes with the right people in the room. It saves 2-6 weeks on the back end.

1. Who Needs to Be in the Scoping Room

If you run this checklist alone, or with only your operations lead, you will miss roughly half the access questions. Document automation touches infrastructure, compliance, and workflow, and those three areas rarely live in the same head.

The minimum viable scoping session has three roles present, even if two of them are the same person in a smaller shop:

  • Workflow owner: the person who actually processes the documents today. Usually ops manager, office manager, or a senior admin. Knows what the documents are, where they come from, and what "done" looks like.
  • Infrastructure owner: the person who controls network access, user accounts, and VPN provisioning. Could be internal IT, an MSP, or your office manager if you run a 15-person firm on Google Workspace.
  • Compliance or security sign-off: whoever says yes or no to new service accounts, API keys, and third-party access. In regulated industries this is a distinct role. In most 11-100 employee service businesses, this collapses into the owner or a fractional CISO.

If your infrastructure owner is an external MSP, build their response time into the project timeline. A 48-hour ticket SLA on VPN changes will add 10-15 business days to any non-trivial document automation build.

2. Layer One — Document Access

This is where the majority of projects stall. Before anyone writes a trigger, a parser, or a signing flow, you need a definitive answer to one question: can an automation process actually reach these documents without a human in the loop?

2.1 Where do the documents actually live?

  • Local network server, on-premise, reachable only from office LAN
  • Cloud server requiring VPN tunnel
  • Mainstream cloud storage (Google Drive, SharePoint, OneDrive, Dropbox)
  • Inside a proprietary vertical application (agency management system, ERP, practice management software, claims platform)
  • Email inboxes (shared mailbox, distribution list, individual user inbox)
  • Multiple locations simultaneously — this is the most common answer and the most expensive

2.2 What does a human need to do today to open one?

Write it down step by step. Every step is an access requirement the automation will also need to satisfy, or replace with something equivalent.

2.4 The five questions that decide the timeline

  • Service account availability: can IT or the software vendor issue a non-human account with scoped permissions? If no, you are building RPA on a human account, which is fragile and a compliance risk.
  • IP whitelisting: does the source system require traffic from a specific IP range? If yes, your automation host needs a static IP or a NAT gateway, which adds infrastructure cost.
  • MFA handling: is multi-factor authentication enforced on the account the automation will use? If yes, you need app-password support, conditional access exemptions, or a TOTP-compatible flow.
  • Session persistence: how long does a login last before re-auth is required? Anything under 8 hours means you need re-auth logic built into the workflow.
  • Audit requirements: does every document access need to be logged against a named user? If yes, you may be blocked from using a service account entirely.

If you cannot answer all five of these in the scoping session, stop scoping. Send someone to get the answers before you quote. A $15,000 quote against unknown answers becomes a $25,000 change order.

3. Layer Two — Workflow Logic

Only after Layer One is nailed down does it make sense to map the actual workflow. This is the part that shows up in sales demos. It is also the part that is genuinely fast to build once access is solved.

3.1 Trigger definition

  • Event-driven trigger: a new document appears in a folder, an email arrives with an attachment, a record is created in the source system.
  • Schedule-driven trigger: every 15 minutes, every night at 2 AM, every Monday at 9 AM.
  • Human-initiated trigger: someone clicks a button, sends an email to an alias, or drops a file in a Slack channel.

Event-driven triggers are always cheaper to operate but require webhook support or reliable polling. If the source system has neither, you are back to schedule-driven, which means latency and more duplicate-detection logic.

3.2 Processing steps

For each document type in scope, write out the transformation chain. A typical insurance signing flow looks like this:

  1. Detect new policy document in agency management system
  2. Extract client identifier and policy number from filename or metadata
  3. Match against CRM to find signer email and phone
  4. Generate signing request in DocuSign or equivalent
  5. Attach completed signed PDF back to the agency management system
  6. Notify account manager via Slack with link to signed copy
  7. Log completion in audit table

Seven steps. Each one has an error state. Each error state needs a decision: retry, alert, or dead-letter queue. Scope the error handling, not just the happy path.

4. Layer Three — Compliance and Handoff

The third layer is the one most teams skip until the build is 90% done, then discover they need to redo half the work to pass an audit. Front-load it.

  • Data residency: where does the document physically pass through? If your documents must not leave a specific country or cloud region, this constrains your stack from day one. Self-hosted n8n on a regional VPS solves this. Most SaaS automation tools do not.
  • PII and PHI handling: does the document contain personally identifiable information or protected health information? If yes, you need encryption at rest, encryption in transit, scoped access, and an audit log. This is not optional.
  • Retention policy: how long can the automation retain a copy of the document? Many compliance regimes require that processing systems not hold copies beyond the processing window. This affects your database schema.
  • Audit trail ownership: who owns the log of every automated action? If your automation runs under a service account, the audit trail lives in the automation layer, not in the source system. Confirm this is acceptable to your compliance owner.
  • Failure disclosure: when the automation fails, who gets notified, and how fast? A 4-hour silent failure on an insurance signing flow is a regulatory incident in some jurisdictions.

Compliance questions look boring in a scoping session. They become expensive in a penetration test. Answer them before you build, not after.

5. Quoting Against the Checklist

Once all three layers are answered, you can produce a quote that will not blow up. The breakdown I use on every engagement looks like this:

Line ItemTypical Share of BudgetNotes
Access layer setup35-50%VPN, service accounts, API credentials, IP whitelisting, test access
Workflow build20-30%The automation itself — triggers, processing, notifications
Error handling and retries10-15%Dead-letter queues, alerting, replay tooling
Compliance and audit logging10-15%Encryption, retention, audit tables
Documentation and handoff5-10%Runbooks, credentials rotation procedure, ownership matrix

If a vendor quote shows 70% of the budget going to "workflow build" and almost nothing to access or compliance, that quote is wrong. Not maliciously wrong — they just have not priced in the work that is about to eat their timeline and yours.

6. The Five Red Flags That Mean You Are Not Ready to Build

  • "We will figure out access during the build." You will not. Stop and figure it out first, or accept a 2-3x timeline overrun.
  • "Our IT guy said he can get us a login." A personal login is not a service account. Automations running on personal logins are fragile, non-compliant, and impossible to audit cleanly.
  • "The software vendor has not replied about API access yet." Until they do, you do not know whether this project is a 3-day API build or a 4-week RPA build. Do not quote yet.
  • "We don't know the volume, probably not a lot." Measure it. Count documents for one week. The stack changes at each tier boundary in the table above.
  • "Compliance is not really a concern for us." It becomes a concern the first time a signed document leaks. Answer the Layer Three questions even if the answers are all "no, not required."

7. How to Run the 45-Minute Scoping Session

Book one calendar block, the three roles from section 1 in the room, this checklist on screen. Run it in order: Layer One, Layer Two, Layer Three. Do not let the conversation jump ahead to "what will the automation do" before Layer One is complete, because the answers to Layer One change what is buildable in Layer Two.

Output of the session should be a one-page document with:

  1. Document sources and access methods, with owners named for each
  2. Trigger, processing steps, and error states, with volume estimate
  3. Compliance requirements and audit-log destination
  4. Open questions that block quoting, with a named owner and a due date for each

That one-pager is what you send to vendors to get realistic bids, or what you hand to your internal engineering lead to scope the build. Anything less detailed than that is a wish, not a scope.
