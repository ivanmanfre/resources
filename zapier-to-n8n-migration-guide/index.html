Why This Migration Matters Right Now

Zapier's per-task pricing is brilliantly designed to punish success. The more your business grows, the more your automation bill grows, and the math gets ugly fast. A workflow firing 10,000 tasks a month on Zapier's Professional plan costs you roughly $73/month before you add any teammates or premium apps. Run that same workflow on a $20/month Hetzner box with n8n and your marginal cost per task is functionally zero.

But the cost is only half the story. The bigger problem is what you can't build on Zapier. Real branching logic, AI agent workflows, custom database queries, webhook orchestration with retries, multi-tenant routing — these either don't exist on Zapier or they exist as crippled versions that make you fight the platform. n8n removes the ceiling.

If you're running more than 15 active Zaps or paying over $200/month, you're past the breakeven point. Migration pays for itself inside 60 days.

Score Each Workflow on Migration Difficulty

  • Easy (1 point): Single trigger, 1-2 actions, standard apps (Gmail, Slack, Sheets, HubSpot).
  • Medium (3 points): 3-5 steps, includes Formatter or Filter, uses common but slightly niche apps.

Total your scores. Anything under 30 total points is a one-weekend migration. Anything over 80 points needs a phased plan with rollback windows.

The Mental Model Shift

Zapier thinks in single records. Each Zap run handles one item. n8n thinks in arrays — every node receives a list of items and processes them in parallel. This trips up new users constantly.

  • Zapier mindset: "When a new lead arrives, send a Slack message and add to HubSpot."
  • n8n mindset: "When leads arrive, transform the array, route by region, batch the HubSpot calls, fan out the Slack notifications."

Once you stop fighting the array model, you start writing one workflow that replaces five Zaps.

5. Migrate in Three Waves, Never All At Once

The single biggest mistake I see: teams try to migrate everything in a weekend. Then a critical workflow breaks on Monday morning, the CEO loses faith, and the project gets shelved for six months.

Wave 1: Low-Risk Workflows (Week 1)

  • What to migrate: Internal notifications, archival jobs, anything where a 24-hour outage costs nothing.
  • Why first: You build platform muscle without business risk.
  • Examples: Slack-to-Notion archive, daily metric digests, internal birthday reminders.

Wave 2: Operational Workflows (Weeks 2-3)

  • What to migrate: Lead routing, form processing, calendar reminders, customer success touchpoints.
  • Why second: A short outage is recoverable, but you need monitoring in place.
  • Run both in parallel for 7 days. Compare execution logs. Flip the trigger source only after a clean week.

Wave 3: Revenue-Critical Workflows (Weeks 4+)

  • What to migrate: Invoicing, payment confirmations, contract delivery, anything tied to cash.
  • Why last: By now you trust the platform and have a runbook for failures.
  • Always run in parallel for 14 days minimum with reconciliation reports.

The parallel-run period is non-negotiable. Pause your Zapier task counter, leave the Zap active, and compare outputs daily. The cost of two weeks of overlap is a rounding error compared to a missed invoice.

6. Build the Migration Runbook

For every workflow you migrate, document the same five things. This is what separates a clean migration from a six-month death march.

  1. Trigger source: Where data enters. Webhook URL, polling schedule, or app event.
  2. Data shape: Sample payload from a real Zapier run. Paste it into the n8n trigger as test data.
  3. Side effects: Every external system the workflow writes to. Track these for the parallel-run reconciliation.
  4. Failure mode: What happens when an API errors out. Retry policy, dead-letter queue, alert channel.
  5. Owner: Named human who gets paged when it breaks. Not a team alias. A person.

7. Avoid the Five Migration Traps I See Most Often

Every migration I've done for a service business hits at least one of these. Knowing them in advance saves a week of debugging.

  • Webhook URL drift: Zapier webhook URLs are baked into upstream apps (Typeform, Stripe, Calendly). When you migrate, you have to update every upstream config — and the team that owns those apps usually isn't the team running the migration.
  • Credential reuse: Don't reuse OAuth tokens between Zapier and n8n. Create fresh app connections in n8n with their own tokens. Otherwise revoking access in one platform kills the other.
  • Timezone defaults: Zapier defaults to your account timezone. n8n defaults to UTC. Schedule triggers fire at the wrong hour and nobody notices until a Monday morning report goes out at 3am.
  • Rate limits: n8n is fast enough to hit API rate limits that Zapier was naturally throttling. Use the n8n "Loop Over Items" node with a wait or the built-in batching options on HTTP Request.
  • Silent failures: Zapier emails you when a Zap turns off. n8n doesn't. Set up a workflow that pings every active workflow's status weekly and posts to Slack. Took me getting burned twice to make this standard.

The credential trap is the most expensive one. I've seen a team revoke a Zapier HubSpot connection while decommissioning, not realize the n8n connection used the same OAuth grant, and lose four hours of inbound leads on a Tuesday.
