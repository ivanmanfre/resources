Why This Matters

Here's a scenario I see all the time. A deal closes in HubSpot. Someone on the ops team gets a notification. They open ClickUp (or Asana, or Monday, or whatever), try to figure out if the client already exists, maybe search around for a few minutes, and then either create a new folder or dump the project into an existing one. Sometimes they get it wrong. Sometimes a client ends up with three folders because three different people created them on three different days.

The real problem isn't laziness or carelessness. It's that nobody wrote down the rules. When you ask most teams "how do you decide where a new project goes?", the answer is basically "we just know." That works when you have five clients. It falls apart at fifty.

I built a system for a client that routes new deals from HubSpot into ClickUp automatically, creating folders, matching existing clients, and handling edge cases like white-label relationships and parent companies. This guide is the decision logic behind that system. Whether you automate it or just use it as a manual reference, the framework is the same.

Section 1: The Two-Stage Routing Model

Before you can route anything, you need to understand when routing happens. I use a two-stage model:

Stage 1: Deal closes in CRM. When a deal hits "closed won" (or whatever your trigger status is), you create a record in your project management tool. This is just the initial entry, not the full project setup.

Stage 2: Status change triggers folder creation. When someone changes that record to "onboarding" (or your equivalent), the system populates the delivery space with the actual folder structure, templates, task lists, whatever your team needs.

Why two stages? Because not every closed deal is ready for project setup immediately. The first stage captures it. The second stage builds it out. This also gives your team a checkpoint to verify the data before folders get created.

Action Item: Define your two trigger points. What CRM status creates the initial record? What status triggers the full folder build?

Section 2: New Client vs. Existing Client Detection

This is the first fork in your routing logic. When a new deal comes in, you need to answer one question: does this client already have a folder?

There are two ways to figure this out:

Option A: Use the CRM deal type field. If your CRM has a field like "deal type" with values like "new business" and "existing business," that's your cleanest signal. A field is always more reliable than parsing text. I tell every client the same thing: always trust the field over the deal name. Fields are structured data. Deal names are whatever your sales team typed in that day.

Option B: Fuzzy matching against existing folders. Pull all your current client folders from your project management tool. Pull the deal name or company name from the CRM. Score them against each other for similarity. If the score is above 90%, you've got a match. If not, it's a new client.

Why 90%? Because in practice, you'll see small variations. Maybe the deal says "Bluestream" and the folder says "Blue Stream." Maybe someone abbreviated "University of Tennessee" to "UTK." A 90% threshold catches these without creating false matches.

TBH, the best approach is to use both. Check the deal type field first. If it says "existing business," confirm by fuzzy matching against your folder list. If it says "new business," still run the match just in case someone miscategorized it.

    • Signal: CRM deal type field | Reliability: High | When to Use: Primary check, always
    • Signal: Company name field | Reliability: High | When to Use: Secondary confirmation
    • Signal: Deal title text | Reliability: Medium | When to Use: Fuzzy match fallback
    • Signal: Manual lookup | Reliability: Low | When to Use: Last resort
> Action Item: Check if your CRM has a deal type field (or equivalent). If not, create one with at least two values: "New Business" and "Existing Business." Make sure your sales team uses it consistently.

Section 3: The Parent Company Problem

This is where it gets interesting, and where most teams don't have clear rules.

Some clients are actually sub-clients of a larger company. Think of a university system where you might work with the main campus, the foundation, and three satellite campuses. Or a holding company that sends you work from five different brands.

The question: does each engagement get its own folder, or do they all go into one parent folder?

The rule I use: If the parent organization is the consistent entity across engagements, all projects go into one root folder with sub-folders or lists for individual engagements.

Real example from a client I work with: all University of Tennessee projects go into a single UTK folder. Doesn't matter if it's a website build, an academic calendar, or a foundation project. The team consistently tags deals with "UTK" in the name, and the primary company field in HubSpot shows the university domain. So the routing logic is straightforward: if primary company = UTK, route to the UTK folder.

But there's always an edge case. The UT Foundation is technically a separate entity. It's rare enough that the team decided to handle it manually rather than build a special rule. And that's a valid call. Not everything needs to be automated. If an edge case shows up once or twice a year, just move the folder by hand.

The decision matrix for parent companies:

    • Frequency: Frequent (5+ deals/year) | Client Naming Consistency: High | Recommendation: Automate with parent folder routing
    • Frequency: Frequent | Client Naming Consistency: Low | Recommendation: Fix naming conventions first, then automate
    • Frequency: Rare (1-2 deals/year) | Client Naming Consistency: Any | Recommendation: Handle manually, not worth the automation
> Action Item: List your top 5 clients by deal volume. For each one, check: do they have multiple engagements? Is the naming consistent in your CRM? If yes to both, they're candidates for parent folder routing.

Section 4: White-Label Client Routing

This one is tricky, and I've spent real time working through it with clients.

A white-label relationship means Company A sends you work on behalf of Company B. Company A pays you. Company B is the end client. The question is: where does Company B's project folder go?

The framework I landed on:

  • If the referring company (Company A) is paying you, and the end client (Company B) is a white-label, the project goes into Company A's root folder. Company B becomes a sub-folder or list inside that root.
  • If the end client (Company B) pays you directly, even though they came through Company A, they get their own separate folder.
The trigger rule: check the primary company field in your CRM. If primary company = the referring partner (like "Evenbound"), route to that partner's root folder. If primary company = the end client's own name, create a separate folder.

You can add a backup check: if the deal name contains the referring partner's name, that's another signal it belongs in their root folder.

So yeah, it comes down to: who's paying you? That determines the folder structure.

    • Relationship Type: White-label | Who Pays: Partner pays you | Folder Location: Inside partner's root folder
    • Relationship Type: Referral (direct pay) | Who Pays: End client pays you | Folder Location: Separate client folder
    • Relationship Type: Internal sub-brand | Who Pays: Parent company pays | Folder Location: Inside parent's root folder
The consistency problem: This only works if your sales team fills in the primary company field correctly. I always tell teams: the field is 100% safe. The deal name is a backup. If you're going to build automation off of CRM data, you need to make sure the data entry is reliable. Talk to your BD team. Make it part of their process.
Action Item: Identify all white-label or partner relationships in your current client list. For each one, verify that the primary company field in your CRM reflects the correct entity. If it doesn't, fix it before building any automation.

Section 5: Building Your Routing Rules (The Decision Tree)

Let's put it all together into a decision tree you can actually implement. When a new deal triggers folder creation, here's the logic in order:

Step 1: Check deal type field.
  • "Existing business" -> proceed to Step 2
  • "New business" -> proceed to Step 3
  • Field is empty -> proceed to Step 3 (treat as new, verify with fuzzy match)
Step 2: Find the existing folder.
  • Pull all client folders from your PM tool
  • Fuzzy match the deal's company name against folder names
  • Score above 90%? Route to that folder. Done.
  • Score below 90%? Flag for manual review. Don't auto-create, it might be a misspelling.
Step 3: Check for parent company / white-label rules.
  • Is the primary company field one of your known parent companies? (UTK, Evenbound, etc.)
- Yes -> Route to that parent's root folder - No -> proceed to Step 4
  • Does the deal name contain a known parent company name?
- Yes -> Route to that parent's root folder - No -> proceed to Step 4 Step 4: Create new client folder.
  • Use the primary company name (not the deal name) as the folder name
  • Apply your standard folder template
  • Pull in any additional data from the CRM (account manager, project type, etc.)
Action Item: Map this decision tree for your specific setup. Write down every parent company and white-label partner that needs a special routing rule. Keep this list somewhere your automation can reference it, like a lookup table or a dedicated CRM field.

Section 6: The Fields That Matter Most

Your routing logic is only as good as your CRM data. Here are the fields I'd make sure are filled in on every deal:

  • Primary company: The single most reliable routing signal. Use it.
  • Deal type: New business vs. existing business. Simple and powerful.
  • Deal name: Useful as a fuzzy match fallback, but never your primary trigger. People get creative with naming.
  • Deal source: Can help identify referral/partner relationships, but often inconsistently filled in.
  • Account manager: Not a routing field, but pull it into your PM tool so the right person gets assigned automatically.
One more thing: if you have a "white-label" or "partner" tag in your CRM, use it. You can label white-label clients directly in HubSpot (or wherever), and then your automation checks that tag instead of relying on name matching. Tags and fields beat text parsing every time.
    • Field: Primary company | Routing Use: Main routing signal | Reliability: High (if enforced)
    • Field: Deal type | Routing Use: New vs. existing | Reliability: High (if enforced)
    • Field: Deal name | Routing Use: Fuzzy matching fallback | Reliability: Medium
    • Field: White-label tag | Routing Use: Partner routing | Reliability: High (if used)
    • Field: Deal source | Routing Use: Referral identification | Reliability: Low-Medium
> Action Item: Audit your last 20 closed deals. How many have the primary company field filled in? How many have a deal type? If completion is below 90%, you need a process fix before you build automation on top of this data.

Section 7: What to Do When It Goes Wrong

Even with solid routing logic, things will break. A deal gets miscategorized. A fuzzy match picks the wrong client. A new white-label partner shows up that isn't in your rules yet.

The fix should be simple: just drag the folder (or list) to the correct location in your PM tool. This isn't a big deal. Tools like ClickUp make it easy to relocate folders and lists without losing any data.

The real question is: how often is it going wrong?

  • Once or twice a month: That's fine. Handle it manually. Don't over-engineer.
  • Once a week: Look at the pattern. Is it the same type of deal? The same sales rep? Fix the input, not the automation.
  • Daily: Your routing logic has a gap. Go back to your decision tree and add the missing rule.
I always tell clients: if the edge case is rare enough that you'd forget about it between occurrences, leave it manual. Automation is for the stuff that happens repeatedly. Manual handling is for the stuff that happens once in a blue moon.
Action Item: Set up a simple log (even just a shared doc) where your team notes every time they have to manually move a folder. Review it monthly. If patterns emerge, update your routing rules.

Putting It All Together

The whole framework boils down to four questions asked in order:

1. Is this an existing client? (Check deal type field, then fuzzy match)
2. Is this a parent company situation? (Check primary company against your parent company list)
3. Is this a white-label? (Check primary company and deal name for partner indicators)
4. None of the above? (Create a new folder using the primary company name)

That's it. Four questions. The complexity isn't in the logic, it's in making sure your CRM data is clean enough to answer those questions reliably.

The best investment you can make before building any of this into an automation is spending an afternoon with your BD or sales team, making sure they know which fields matter and why. A perfectly designed routing system will fail if the data feeding it is garbage.

Your Next 7 Days

Day 1-2: Audit your CRM data.
Pull your last 30 closed deals. Check completion rates for primary company, deal type, and deal name. Note any inconsistencies.

Day 3: Map your parent companies and white-label partners.
Make a list of every client that should route to a shared root folder. Write down the trigger rules for each one (primary company = X, or deal name contains Y).

Day 4: Draft your decision tree.
Use the template from Section 5 as a starting point. Customize it for your specific setup. Get your ops lead to review it.

Day 5: Talk to your sales/BD team.
Share the fields that matter (Section 6). Agree on a process for consistent data entry. This conversation is more valuable than any automation you'll build.

Day 6: Test with recent deals.
Take 10 recent deals and run them through your decision tree manually. Did they route correctly? Where did the logic break?

Day 7: Decide on automation vs. manual.
If you're handling 5+ new deals per week, automation makes sense. If it's 2-3, a well-documented manual process might be all you need. Either way, the decision framework is the same.
