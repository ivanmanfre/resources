Checklist Title

The Operations Audit Checklist for Manufacturing Companies Under 200 Employees

Use Case

For manufacturing owners, ops directors, and plant managers who know things aren't running as tight as they should but can't pinpoint where the real problems are. Use this before investing in new systems, hiring ops consultants, or buying automation tools. It gives you a clear map of what's working, what's held together with duct tape, and what needs attention first.

Section 1: Order Intake and Job Routing

This is where most manufacturing companies start losing time and don't even realize it. How orders come in, how they get assigned, and how many people have to touch them before work actually starts.

  • [ ] 1.1 Orders come in through a single, consistent channel (not scattered across email, phone, text, and walk-ins)
  • [ ] 1.2 Every order is logged in one system before any work begins (no jobs running off verbal instructions or sticky notes)
  • [ ] 1.3 Job routing to the correct department, machine, or team happens based on clear rules, not tribal knowledge that lives in one person's head
  • [ ] 1.4 You can tell me right now how many times a single order gets manually re-entered or copied between systems (and that number is documented, not guessed)

Section 2: Production Tracking and Floor Visibility

If you can't see what's happening on the floor in real time, you're managing by gut feel. That works until it doesn't, and at 50+ employees it usually doesn't.

  • [ ] 2.1 There is a live view (digital or physical board) showing which jobs are in progress, which are queued, and which are blocked
  • [ ] 2.2 Operators log job status changes as they happen, not at the end of shift or "when they get around to it"
  • [ ] 2.3 Downtime events (machine breakdown, material shortage, waiting on QC) are tracked with a reason code, not just "we lost two hours somewhere"
  • [ ] 2.4 You can pull a report showing actual production time vs. estimated time for any job from the last 30 days without asking three people

Section 3: Data Flow Between Systems

This is the one that kills companies quietly. You have an ERP, maybe a separate quoting tool, maybe spreadsheets for scheduling, maybe a shipping platform. The question is whether data moves between them or whether your team is the integration layer.

  • [ ] 3.1 Customer data exists in one source of truth (not duplicated across quoting software, ERP, accounting, and a shared drive)
  • [ ] 3.2 When a quote becomes an order, the information carries forward automatically, no one is re-typing specs, quantities, or shipping details
  • [ ] 3.3 Inventory counts update when materials are consumed in production, not only when someone does a manual count
  • [ ] 3.4 Shipping and tracking information flows back to the customer-facing system without someone copy-pasting tracking numbers

Section 4: Exception Handling and Error Recovery

Every operation has exceptions. The question is whether you have a system for handling them or whether every exception becomes a fire drill that pulls your best people off real work.

  • [ ] 4.1 There is a documented process for what happens when a job fails QC (who gets notified, how rework is scheduled, how the customer is updated)
  • [ ] 4.2 Material shortages trigger an alert before they stop production, not after a machine sits idle for an hour
  • [ ] 4.3 Customer change requests mid-production follow a defined path (not "whoever picks up the phone makes a call")
  • [ ] 4.4 You know your current error/rework rate as a percentage and it's tracked monthly, not estimated annually

Section 5: Reporting and Decision-Making

You can't improve what you don't measure, yeah, everyone says that. But the real problem isn't measuring. It's that most manufacturers under 200 employees are measuring the wrong things, or measuring the right things in spreadsheets that are two weeks stale.

  • [ ] 5.1 Leadership has access to a weekly snapshot of production output, on-time delivery rate, and rework percentage without requesting it from someone
  • [ ] 5.2 Labor cost per job (or per unit) is calculated and visible, not buried in payroll that gets reconciled once a month
  • [ ] 5.3 You can identify your top 3 bottleneck stations or processes based on data from the last 90 days (not based on "everyone knows it's that machine")
  • [ ] 5.4 Customer profitability is tracked at the account level so you know which clients actually make you money and which ones burn your capacity

Implementation Priority

If you checked off fewer than half the items, don't panic. That's normal for companies in this size range. Here's where to start:

Priority 1: Section 1, Item 1.2 (Single system for order logging)
Everything downstream depends on clean order data coming in. If jobs are starting from verbal instructions, emails, and texts, nothing else you build will be reliable. Fix the front door first.

Priority 2: Section 3, Item 3.1 (Single source of truth for customer data)
Duplicated customer data across systems is the root cause of most fulfillment errors, wrong shipping addresses, outdated specs, mismatched pricing. Pick one system as the master and make everything else read from it.

Priority 3: Section 4, Item 4.4 (Know your error/rework rate)
You can't build a case for any operational investment, automation or otherwise, without this number. Start tracking it this week. Even a simple tally on a whiteboard is better than guessing.
